Subscription Engine Architecture Research Report1. The Architectural Imperative of a Managed Billing LayerIn the ecosystem of modern financial technology, the orchestration of recurring revenue models demands a strict architectural decoupling of raw payment execution from lifecycle state management. Underlying payment gateways, such as Nomba, provide robust transactional primitives—including the Checkout API, Tokenised Cards, and Charge API—designed exclusively for the secure, point-in-time exchange of fiat currency. However, these payment processors do not inherently govern the temporal, mathematically rigorous, and stateful realities of subscription billing.For platforms and Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) providers, this architectural gap creates significant engineering friction. Downstream product teams are frequently forced to repeatedly construct custom logic to handle recurring intervals, prorated upgrades, involuntary churn mitigation, and complex database multi-tenancy. The solution to this operational bottleneck is the implementation of a dedicated Infrastructure Subscriptions Engine.This report outlines the comprehensive architectural blueprint required to construct a managed, multi-tenant subscription layer atop Nomba's transactional primitives. By analyzing the engineering paradigms established by industry-leading platforms such as Stripe Billing, Chargebee, and open-source equivalents like Lago, this document details the implementation of a deterministic state machine, sophisticated failure recovery algorithms (dunning), immutable ledger mathematics for proration, PostgreSQL multi-tenancy via Row-Level Security (RLS), and highly ergonomic developer APIs guarded by strict idempotency. The resulting architecture abstracts the complexities of recurring revenue, empowering downstream development teams to seamlessly monetize their products.2. Finite State-Machine Completeness and Lifecycle OrchestrationA subscription is not a singular transaction; it is an ongoing financial relationship defined by a continuous lifecycle. Consequently, the core of the Subscription Engine is a deterministic finite-state machine (FSM) that dictates the operational status of the subscription from instantiation to termination. The state machine must be strictly enforced, guaranteeing that transitions occur only via cryptographically verifiable external events (e.g., Nomba webhooks) or internal temporal triggers (e.g., asynchronous cron jobs evaluating billing periods).2.1 Taxonomy of Core Subscription StatesIndustry-standard billing engines conceptually categorize subscription states into three macro-classifications: ALIVE (generating revenue or providing trial value), SUSPENDED (awaiting payment resolution or administratively paused), and DEAD (terminal states requiring a new subscription to revive access).The implementation requires the following granular states to ensure complete lifecycle coverage:incomplete: The initial transient state triggered upon creation when the first invoice requires payment. The subscription remains in this state while the customer is directed to the Nomba Checkout URL.incomplete*expired: A terminal DEAD state reached if the initial payment is not captured within a defined window (typically 23 to 24 hours). This state prevents the database from bloating with abandoned checkout attempts and immediately voids the pending initial invoice.trialing: An ALIVE state where the customer is granted full access without immediate charge. The engine automatically transitions the subscription to active and attempts the first charge precisely at the end of the predefined trial period.active: The primary operational ALIVE state. The subscription is in good standing, the most recent invoice has been paid successfully via Nomba, and downstream access is fully granted.past_due: An intermediate SUSPENDED state triggered when a recurring payment attempt (e.g., via Nomba's POST /v1/checkout/tokenized-card-payment) fails. The subscription enters the automated dunning process.unpaid: A terminal or semi-terminal state reached when all automated dunning retries have been exhausted. Future invoices may continue to generate in a draft state for accounting purposes, but automatic collection attempts cease entirely.canceled: A terminal DEAD state indicating the subscription has been manually terminated by the user, the tenant, or automatically post-dunning. No further invoices are generated, and access is definitively revoked.paused: A specialized SUSPENDED state where the subscription temporarily ceases to generate invoices, usually requested by the customer or invoked after a specific workflow.2.2 State Transition ArchitectureThe operational integrity of the billing engine relies on mapping legal transitions between these states based on explicit triggers. It mandates an active asynchronous background worker mechanism—a highly available distributed task queue (e.g., utilizing Redis alongside Celery/Sidekiq, or transactional PostgreSQL-based queues such as Graphile Worker).The worker mechanism continuously evaluates the state of the database. When a subscription reaches its renewal boundary (current_period_end <= NOW()) and the status is active, the system executes a precise sequence of operations:Job Dispatch: An asynchronous task is dispatched and locked to prevent concurrent double-processing of the same renewal event.Invoice Generation: The engine drafts a new Invoice and aggregates all pending InvoiceItems (including baseline plan costs, add-ons, and pending proration adjustments).Token Retrieval: The engine retrieves the stored nomba_token_key, which was securely acquired during the initial Nomba checkout flow by passing tokenizeCard: true.Charge Execution: An idempotent HTTP POST request is made to Nomba's /v1/checkout/tokenized-card-payment utilizing the retrieved token.Reconciliation: The subscription state transitions immediately based on the synchronous response. However, to account for delayed webhook delivery, the engine makes a definitive API verification call to Nomba's /v1/transactions/accounts/single to confirm the true status of the transaction before marking the invoice as paid.3. Dunning Sophistication and Failed-Payment Recovery AlgorithmsPayment failures account for an estimated 20% to 40% of all SaaS customer churn, heavily classifying as involuntary churn. Dunning is the automated, systematic workflow designed to recover these failed transactions through an optimal mix of intelligent retry logic and localized customer communication. The foundational error in rudimentary billing engines is treating all payment failures identically; modern architectures deploy "Smart Retries" that dynamically adapt based on the specific decline code returned by the payment gateway.3.1 Failure Categorization: Soft vs. Hard DeclinesWhen a Nomba charge attempt fails, the underlying issuer decline reason dictates both the mathematical probability of recovery and the required architectural response.Decline CategoryTypical Gateway CodesRecovery ProbabilityArchitectural StrategySoft Declinesinsufficient_funds, processing_error, limit_exceededHigh (40% - 70%)Immediate entry into automated Smart Retry timeline. Wait for temporary holds, daily limits, or payday deposits to clear.Hard Declinescard_expired, lost_card, stolen_card, do_not_honorLow (< 5%)Abort automated retries immediately to avoid network penalty fees. Trigger immediate customer communication directing them to a hosted portal to update payment methods.For instance, insufficient_funds failures frequently resolve without any customer intervention around the 1st or 15th of the month, coinciding with standard payroll cycles. Conversely, retrying a card_expired error repeatedly wastes computational resources and harms merchant standing with card networks.3.2 The Optimal Dunning Timeline (Retry Schedule)Deploying a static, linear retry schedule (e.g., retrying every 24 hours blindly) is statistically inefficient and misses critical temporal recovery windows. Extrapolating from industry metadata analyzing millions of failed transactions, an exponentially spaced scheduling approach autonomously recovers up to 58% of failed payments without the end-user ever realizing a failure occurred.The recommended default Smart Retry schedule for the infrastructure engine is structured as follows:Initial Failure (Day 0): The subscription transitions to past_due. An invoice.payment_failed webhook is dispatched to the tenant. Crucially, if the decline is categorized as soft, no dunning email is dispatched to the end-customer, preventing unnecessary friction.Retry 1 (Day 1 / +24 Hours): Captures transient network errors or rolling 24-hour banking limits. This initial rapid retry historically recovers approximately 22% of failures.Retry 2 (Day 3 / +72 Hours): Targets the clearing of weekend banking holds or incoming deposits. Recovers an additional 19%. At this stage, if the payment fails again, an automated dunning email is triggered containing a secure link to update payment details.Retry 3 (Day 5 / +120 Hours): Targets mid-week banking clearances.Retry 4 (Day 7 / +168 Hours): The final automated attempt, aligning exactly one week after the original failure to capture weekly recurring billing cycles and finalized deposits.Machine learning models within Smart Retries further optimize this schedule by evaluating dynamic variables such as the cardholder's local timezone to execute retries at statistically optimal hours (e.g., 12:01 AM local time).3.3 Grace Periods and Terminal Dunning ActionsThroughout the active dunning period (Days 0 through 7, or optionally extended to 14 or 21 days based on tenant configuration), the subscription remains in the past_due state.A critical architectural paradigm is the strict separation of financial status from entitlement access. Best practices dictate that the downstream SaaS provider should continue granting the user access to the software during the past_due grace period. Immediate cancellation upon an initial failure creates immense user friction; analysis demonstrates that users who lose access immediately are significantly less likely to log in and update their payment credentials.If all retries fail and the configured grace period expires, the engine executes the Terminal Dunning Action. The subscription state is updated to one of two terminal configurations:canceled: The subscription is permanently terminated. All pending invoices are voided, and the revenue is written off.unpaid: The subscription is frozen. New invoices continue to generate in a draft state for accounting and legal purposes, but automatic collection via Nomba ceases entirely. This is heavily utilized in enterprise B2B contracts where the legal obligation to pay persists regardless of software access.Following the terminal action, the engine emits a subscription.canceled or subscription.unpaid webhook, definitively signaling the downstream tenant to permanently revoke application access.4. Proration Mathematics and Ledger ImmutabilityProration—the calculation of partial billing credits and debits when a subscription is upgraded, downgraded, or canceled mid-cycle—represents the most mathematically demanding and architecturally complex component of a billing engine. The foundational principle required to build a compliant billing architecture is absolute immutability: an invoice or line item, once finalized, must never be mutated.4.1 Immutable Invoice Items and the Append-Only LedgerRather than destructively mutating a subscription record and attempting to calculate cost differences dynamically, the engine must employ an append-only ledger model relying entirely on discrete Invoice Items.When a mid-cycle subscription change occurs, the engine does not retroactively alter the previously paid invoice. Instead, it generates two new pending, immutable Invoice Items that will be appended to the next billing cycle's invoice (or billed immediately if the tenant requests an instant charge):A Proration Credit (Negative Item): Mathematically reimbursing the customer for the unused portion of the old plan.A Proration Debit (Positive Item): Charging the customer for the remaining time on the newly selected plan.By defining these as discrete records in the database, the engine guarantees a perfectly auditable trail, massively simplifies tax calculations across different jurisdictions, and ensures that highly complex state changes (e.g., a customer upgrading, downgrading, and adding an add-on all within a single week) resolve mathematically without introducing edge-case race conditions.4.2 The Mathematical Framework for ProrationLeading engines calculate proration down to the exact second by utilizing Unix timestamps, ensuring absolute mathematical precision across billing boundaries. The formula for determining the proportional fraction of time—the proration factor—is:$$\text{Proration Factor} = \frac{T*{\text{end}} - T*{\text{event}}}{T*{\text{end}} - T*{\text{start}}}$$Where:$T*{\text{start}}$ is the Unix timestamp representing the exact start of the current billing cycle.$T_{\text{end}}$ is the Unix timestamp representing the exact end of the current billing cycle.$T_{\text{event}}$ is the Unix timestamp when the upgrade or downgrade event occurred.The monetary adjustments are subsequently calculated as:Credit Amount = $-(\text{Old Plan Price} \times \text{Proration Factor})$Debit Amount = $\text{New Plan Price} \times \text{Proration Factor}$Crucial Architecture Note: In all financial database operations, monetary values must be stored exclusively as integers representing the lowest currency denominator (e.g., kobo for NGN, cents for USD). This eliminates disastrous floating-point arithmetic errors inherent in fractional computing.4.3 Practical Execution: A $10 to $30 Mid-Cycle UpgradeConsider a scenario where a downstream user is on a monthly billing cycle that spans exactly 30 days.Old Plan: $10.00/month (Stored in DB as 1,000 cents)New Plan: $30.00/month (Stored in DB as 3,000 cents)Event: The user initiates an upgrade exactly halfway through the billing cycle (Day 15).The engine dynamically calculates the remaining time fraction (the Proration Factor):
$$\text{Factor} = \frac{30 - 15}{30} = 0.5$$The engine then generates the following immutable Invoice Items:Credit for unused time on $10 plan: $-(1,000 \times 0.5) = -500 \text{ cents} \ (-$5.00)$Debit for remaining time on $30 plan: $3,000 \times 0.5 = 1,500 \text{ cents} \ ($15.00)$Net Immediate Charge to the Customer: $-5.00 + 15.00 = 10.00$.Depending on the configuration parameters passed by the tenant, the engine handles the financial execution in one of two ways. If configured to always_invoice, an immediate invoice for $10.00 is generated and a synchronous call to Nomba's POST /v1/checkout/tokenized-card-payment is dispatched to capture funds. If configured to defer (the standard default), these two items sit as pending ledger entries. When the billing cycle renews on Day 30, the items are rolled into the standard $30.00 renewal invoice, resulting in a total combined invoice of $40.00.5. Multi-Tenant Cleanliness and PostgreSQL Schema DesignAs an infrastructure layer, the Subscription Engine inherently operates as a B2B2C or B2B2B model. It serves development teams (tenants), who in turn utilize the engine to bill their own end-users (customers). Strict data isolation is a foundational security and compliance mandate; a cross-tenant data leak where one startup sees another startup's financial data is a catastrophic, platform-ending event.5.1 The Pooled Isolation Strategy via Row-Level Security (RLS)Architecturally, multi-tenancy can be approached via Physical Isolation (Silo: one database per tenant), Logical Schema Isolation (Schema: one Postgres schema per tenant), or Pooled Isolation (one shared schema for all tenants).While Silo and Schema approaches provide robust physical boundaries, they introduce severe operational friction at scale. Managing 10,000 discrete database migrations for 10,000 SaaS tenants creates massive deployment bottlenecks and limits database connection pooling efficiency. Therefore, the optimal architectural choice for the vast majority of SaaS infrastructure is the Pooled Isolation Model within PostgreSQL, cryptographically reinforced by Row-Level Security (RLS).In a Pooled Model, every tenant-scoped table contains a tenant_id foreign key. Relying solely on application-level ORM filters (e.g., ensuring developers always write WHERE tenant_id = ?) is dangerously insufficient; human error will inevitably lead to a missed clause and subsequent data leak. RLS shifts the enforcement of isolation out of the application code and deep into the database engine itself.Implementing RLSThe application middleware is configured to inject the tenant context into the PostgreSQL session variables immediately upon checking out a connection from the pool (e.g., via PgBouncer or the native framework pool):SQL-- Application middleware sets the context before executing any queries
SET LOCAL app.current_tenant = 'tenant_uuid_here';
RLS policies are then strictly defined on every table to automatically append the isolation clause to every SELECT, INSERT, UPDATE, and DELETE operation:SQLALTER TABLE subscriptions ENABLE ROW LEVEL SECURITY;

CREATE POLICY tenant_isolation_policy ON subscriptions
USING (tenant_id = current_setting('app.current_tenant')::UUID)
WITH CHECK (tenant_id = current_setting('app.current_tenant')::UUID);
This structural guarantee ensures that even if a developer maliciously or accidentally executes SELECT _ FROM subscriptions;, the PostgreSQL database engine intercepts the query and safely evaluates it as SELECT _ FROM subscriptions WHERE tenant_id = 'tenant_uuid_here';.5.2 High-Level Relational Database Schema (DDL)The foundational PostgreSQL schema requires rigorous normalization to separate Identity data, Product catalogs, and the Financial Ledger. Emulating the gold standard set by platforms like Stripe, the Engine's API must exhibit absolute structural predictability, deliver highly actionable error responses, and provide robust mechanisms for safely navigating the unreliable nature of distributed networks.6.1 Critical API SurfaceThe engine exposes a RESTful interface to downstream tenants. Resource IDs must strictly utilize type-prefixed strings (e.g., cus_8j3k9..., sub_9m2x..., inv_1l0z...) rather than raw database UUIDs. This design pattern reduces cognitive overhead for developers, allowing immediate contextual recognition during debugging and log analysis.The primary integration path requires only a streamlined set of core endpoints:POST /v1/customers: Provisions a customer entity within the tenant's isolated data silo.POST /v1/subscriptions: Attaches a plan to a customer. If immediate payment is required, this returns an incomplete subscription alongside a dynamically generated nomba_checkout_url. The tenant redirects their user to this link. Under the hood, the engine calls Nomba's POST /v1/checkout/order and injects tokenizeCard: true to ensure the card is vaulted securely for future recurring billing cycles.POST /v1/customer-portal/sessions: Generates an authenticated, short-lived URL to a hosted React/Next.js portal. This self-serve ecosystem empowers end-users to update expired cards, view past invoices, or initiate cancellations without requiring the tenant to write any UI code.POST /v1/webhooks: The critical entry point for Nomba to communicate asynchronously with the Engine. Specifically, it listens to Nomba's payment_success payload to drive the state machine from incomplete to active, or to successfully resolve a past_due dunning state.6.2 Designing for Idempotency in Distributed FinanceIn distributed financial systems, network partitions, client timeouts, and server crashes are inevitable. If a tenant issues a POST /v1/subscriptions request to charge a customer, but the internet connection drops before the HTTP 200 OK response is received, the tenant is left in a state of perilous ambiguity. Retrying the request blindly could easily result in double-billing the customer.To eradicate this risk, every mutating endpoint (all POST, PUT, and PATCH requests) must mandate or heavily encourage the inclusion of an Idempotency-Key HTTP header (typically a V4 UUID generated randomly by the client).Implementation via Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) and PostgresIdempotency is implemented via a dedicated idempotency_keys table designed to safely manage concurrent mutations and cache successful responses.SQLCREATE TABLE idempotency_keys (
id UUID PRIMARY KEY DEFAULT gen_random_uuid(),
tenant_id UUID NOT NULL REFERENCES tenants(id),
idempotency_key VARCHAR(255) NOT NULL,
locked_at TIMESTAMP WITH TIME ZONE,
recovery_point VARCHAR(50) DEFAULT 'started',
request_params JSONB NOT NULL,
response_code INT,
response_body JSONB,
created_at TIMESTAMP WITH TIME ZONE DEFAULT NOW(),
UNIQUE (tenant_id, idempotency_key)
);
When a request arrives bearing an Idempotency Key, the engine performs the following lifecycle:PhaseDescription of OperationEngine Action1. Creation / LockingThe engine attempts to insert the Idempotency-Key into the database. If it exists, it checks the locked_at timestamp. If it is currently locked, another thread is actively processing it (returns HTTP 409 Conflict). If it exists and possesses a response_body, the engine returns the cached response immediately, bypassing all further logic entirely.Database read/write.2. Atomic MutationThe engine begins a local database transaction. It mutates internal state (e.g., generating the Invoice and Subscription rows within the local PostgreSQL instance).Local state change.3. Update Recovery PointThe engine updates the recovery_point column to internal_state_saved and explicitly commits the local transaction.Checkpoint creation.4. Foreign State MutationThe engine executes the external API call (e.g., communicating over the network with Nomba's POST /v1/checkout/tokenized-card-payment). External network calls must never occur inside a database transaction, as a slow API call will exhaust the database connection pool.External network call.5. CompletionUpon receiving the response from Nomba, the engine updates the subscription state, saves the final response_body and response_code to the idempotency_keys table, and releases the lock (locked_at = NULL). The response is returned to the tenant.Final state cache.If a server crashes exactly at Phase 4, the tenant eventually times out and retries the request using the same key. The engine reads the recovery_point, realizes the internal database rows were already created during the previous attempt, safely skips Phase 2, and cleanly resumes execution at Phase 4. This architecture guarantees that a customer is charged exactly once, regardless of network volatility.7. Synthesizing the Integration with NombaThe architecture outlined throughout this report functions as a sophisticated orchestration layer that abstracts Nomba's raw, robust transaction APIs. By mapping the engine's internal entities to Nomba's requirements, the system effectively bridges the gap between recurring business logic and actual fiat currency movement.Initialization via Checkout: Downstream checkout links are generated by the engine invoking Nomba's POST /v1/checkout/order. The engine critically injects tokenizeCard: true to ensure the customer's card details are vaulted by the gateway for future recurring rebilling, and assigns its internal invoice ID directly to Nomba's orderReference to ensure bi-directional tracking.Lifecycle Billing Execution: At the exact current_period_end of a subscription, the Engine's asynchronous cron worker extracts the saved nomba_token_key and dispatches it to Nomba's POST /v1/checkout/tokenized-card-payment to execute the recurring charge seamlessly.Aggressive Verification: Because HTTP webhooks can occasionally be delayed by network latency or dropped entirely, the Engine aggressively verifies the status of any ambiguous transaction by actively querying Nomba's GET /v1/transactions/accounts/single?transactionRef={reference}. This server-side polling prevents premature, erroneous transitions into the past_due dunning state and protects against critical delivery failures.By leveraging immutable proration mathematics, asynchronous workers for lifecycle state management, intelligent ML-driven dunning intervals, and multi-tenant RLS isolation, this Subscription Engine reliably elevates Nomba's transactional primitives into a comprehensive, enterprise-grade recurring revenue platform.
